## 0. Next Focus for Building Agent


### Final Product Direction


Rally should focus next on:


> Dynamic Home + LA badminton/pickleball closed beta + Regulars groups + lightweight in-group tournaments.


Do **not** jump directly into full Teams, full Leagues, payments, or external city expansion yet. Those are later once the weekly play loop is proven.


### Immediate Build Priority


- [ ] Replace any single-purpose homepage thinking with a **Dynamic Home** screen.

- [ ] Keep bottom navigation simple: `Home | Discover | Host | Chats | Profile`.

- [ ] Treat **Regulars** as a lightweight group/crew system, not a formal team or league.

- [ ] Allow Regulars groups to support **multiple sports**.

- [ ] Focus beta copy on **LA badminton and pickleball**.

- [ ] Add a "Bring Rally to your city" / partnership interest CTA, but do not expand cities yet.

- [ ] Add founder benefits language without promising unlimited lifetime Plus to everyone.

- [ ] Move **Mini Tournaments inside Regulars** earlier in the roadmap.

- [ ] Delay full Teams until Regulars groups repeat.

- [ ] Delay full Leagues until teams and local demand exist.


### Product Bet


Existing 50-person badminton group chats already work for communication. Rally must give those groups something chat cannot do well:


- RSVP into limited court spots.

- Schedule recurring games.

- Track attendance and reliability.

- Run mini tournaments.

- Rotate partners fairly.

- Keep score history.

- Make the group more fun and competitive.


This is the strongest near-term wedge.


### Beta Positioning


Use clear expectation-setting:


```text
Rally Beta is currently focused on LA badminton and pickleball.

Want to host games, test Rally with your group, or bring Rally to your city?

Contact us.
```


### Founder Benefits


Use careful language:


```text
Active beta testers, hosts, and city partners may receive Founding Member benefits after launch.

Benefits may include extended Plus access, founder badges, host perks, or discounted organizer tools.
```


Do not promise "Plus for life" broadly. Reserve stronger lifetime benefits only for verified hosts or city partners who materially help growth.


## 1. Product Review


### Product Summary


**Rally** is a mobile sports coordination platform for helping people find, host, join, and repeatedly organize amateur sports games.


The current app is already moving in the right direction:


- Discovery for nearby games.

- Game creation.

- Game Room chat.

- Cost notes.

- Announcements.

- RSVP.

- Regulars/crew flow.

- Roster finalization.

- Trust/review foundations.


The strongest long-term positioning is:


> Rally is the operating system for recreational and competitive local sports.


It should not be only a matching app. A pure matching app becomes replaceable once users exchange phone numbers or social handles. Rally becomes durable when it owns the repeat workflow: scheduling, RSVP, attendance, reliability, team history, standings, seasons, and local sports culture.


### End Goal


The end-state product should look like a sports network with three layers:


1. **Player Layer**

- Find games.

- Join games.

- Meet reliable players.

- Build a sports profile.

- Track attendance, reviews, teams, and playing history.


2. **Organizer / Team Layer**

- Manage recurring games.

- RSVP a large group into limited court spots.

- Build rosters.

- Track no-shows.

- Run team pages and team history.


3. **League / Culture Layer**

- Run seasons.

- Track standings.

- Verify results.

- Host playoffs.

- Preserve yearly champion history.

- Build local sports identity by city, sport, region, and skill level.


### Vision


> Make local sports easier to start, easier to repeat, and more meaningful over time.


### Mission


> Help everyday players find reliable people to play with, help organizers run better games, and help local sports communities grow into recurring teams, leagues, and culture.


### Target Audience


#### Primary MVP Audience


- Recreational badminton, pickleball, basketball, soccer, volleyball, tennis, and similar local sports players.

- Players who want to play but do not always have enough people.

- Hosts who already coordinate through WhatsApp, LINE, Instagram, WeChat, Discord, or text groups.

- Local friend groups with irregular attendance.


#### Best Early Adopter


The best early adopter is not the casual player. It is the **host / organizer**.


This person:


- Already has 10-50 people they message.

- Often needs 2-4 more players.

- Gets annoyed by no-shows.

- Wants RSVP clarity.

- Wants to fill games quickly.

- Has influence over many other users.


#### Serious Player Segment


Serious players want:


- Better quality games.

- Skill filtering.

- Reliable teammates.

- Competitive teams.

- League play.

- Public record/history.

- Recognition.


They are more likely to pay than casual users.


### Business Model


The business model should avoid charging for basic social connection. Friend-adding, basic matching, and text chat should remain free because they create liquidity.


Revenue should come from:


- Efficiency.

- Organization.

- Reliability.

- Team operations.

- League operations.

- Competitive history.


#### Recommended Pricing


| Tier | Target Price | Buyer | Core Value |

|---|---:|---|---|

| Free | `$0` | Recreational players | Discovery, joining, text chat, basic profile |

| Player Plus | `$2.99 - $4.99/mo` or `$24.99 - $39.99/yr` | Serious players | Advanced filters, higher limits, reliability insight, priority |

| Team Plan | `$9.99 - $19.99/mo/team` | Captains / recurring teams | Roster, RSVP, attendance, no-show tracking, team page |

| League Plan | `$49 - $199/mo/league` | Gyms, organizers, hosted leagues | Seasons, schedules, standings, playoffs, admin tools |


#### Best Monetization Order


1. Keep player discovery free.

2. Build retention through Regulars and recurring games.

3. Monetize organizers first.

4. Monetize serious players second.

5. Monetize teams third.

6. Monetize leagues after local culture exists.


#### Founder Benefits Strategy


Founder benefits are useful for beta growth, but the promise must be controlled.


Recommended approach:


- Beta testers get free Plus during beta.

- Active players who give feedback may get extended Plus after launch.

- Verified hosts may get founder badges, longer free access, or discounted organizer tools.

- City partners may get special partnership terms if they help launch a real local community.


Avoid a blanket "Plus for life" promise. Use:


```text
Active beta testers and hosts may receive Founding Member benefits after launch.

Contact us if you want to host games or bring Rally to your city.
```


This gives you flexibility while still rewarding early contributors.


### Strategic Positioning


Rally should not position itself as:


- Sports Tinder.

- A generic event app.

- A chat app.

- A league tool only.


Rally should position itself as:


- The place to find reliable players.

- The easiest way to fill a game tonight.

- The easiest way to run recurring games.

- The place where amateur sports history lives.


### Core Product Loop


```text
Need to play

-> Discover game or host game

-> Join / RSVP

-> Chat in Game Room

-> Finalize roster

-> Play

-> Attendance / reviews

-> Regulars or next game

-> Repeat
```


The app wins if this loop repeats weekly.


### Marketplace Liquidity Rule


Do not spread too thin.


Early Rally should focus on:


- One city or region.

- One or two sports.

- A few strong organizers.

- Recurring games, not random one-off discovery.


A thin marketplace across 10 sports and many neighborhoods will feel empty.


### Current Product Direction Review


The current product decisions are mostly correct:


- Deferring full Teams until liquidity exists is correct.

- Deferring payments/fee split is correct.

- Keeping real identity visible is reasonable for sports coordination.

- Making reviews per game and accumulating on profile is correct.

- Building cost note, announcements, RSVP, and Regulars is exactly the right near-term path.


The next product risk is feature sprawl. The product should not become League SaaS before the weekly game loop is strong.


## 2. Screen-by-Screen Product UX


### App Navigation Recommendation


Use a simple bottom tab layout:


```text
Home | Discover | Host | Chats | Profile
```


The homepage should be dynamic. It is not only Discover and not only Chats.


For new or inactive users, Home should emphasize:


```text
Games near you

Host a game

Create or join a Regulars group

Invite friends
```


For active users, Home should emphasize:


```text
Next Up

Confirm you're playing

Active crew chats

Schedule next game

Your crews
```


Regulars should not be a bottom tab at first. Regulars should live inside Home, Chats, Activity Detail, and Profile:


- Home shows upcoming Regulars games and "schedule next game."

- Chats shows Regulars group threads.

- Activity Detail links a game to its Regulars group.

- Profile shows groups the user belongs to.


Later, if Regulars becomes the main retention product, consider a `Groups` tab:


```text
Home | Discover | Host | Groups | Profile
```


Do not use a `Leagues` tab until league usage exists.

## 2.8 Regulars / Crew Screen


### Purpose


Turn one successful game into a repeat group.


Regulars are not formal Teams and not Leagues. Regulars are lightweight friend/crew groups that help people play again.


Regulars can be sport-specific or multi-sport:


- **Sport-specific Regulars**: "LA Badminton Regulars"

- **Multi-sport Regulars**: "Pasadena Weekend Crew" for badminton, pickleball, basketball, tennis, etc.


Multi-sport support matters because real friend groups often try different sports together. The group should have a default sport, but games inside the group can choose any supported sport.

Regulars should come before formal Teams.

### Regulars vs Chat


Regulars should be more than another chat thread.


Regulars should provide:


- Member list.

- Upcoming games.

- RSVP.

- Schedule next game.

- Attendance history.

- Reliability signal.

- Mini tournaments.

- Invite link.


Chat is only one surface inside Regulars, not the product itself.

## 3. High-Level Advice


### 3.1 Do Not Build Too Broad Too Early


The current app already supports many sports, but growth should not happen across all sports at once.


Recommended launch constraint:


- 1 city/region.

- 1 primary sport.

- 5-10 organizers.

- 50-200 early players.

- Weekly repeat games.


Expand only after the weekly loop works.


### 3.2 Build Around Hosts


Hosts create supply. Supply creates player demand.


Prioritize features that make hosts more successful:


- Invite link.

- RSVP.

- Attendance.

- Waitlist.

- Broadcast.

- Schedule next game.

- Fill missing spots.

- Reliability insight.


### 3.3 Treat Regulars as the Core Retention Product


The best signal is not downloads. It is:


> Did a game produce another game?


Track:


- Game created.

- Players joined.

- Roster finalized.

- Game happened.

- Reviews/attendance submitted.

- Regulars group created.

- Next game scheduled.

- Same group plays again.


### 3.4 Delay Formal Teams Until Pull Exists


Teams are emotionally appealing, but they add complexity:


- Roles.

- Rosters.

- Records.

- Disputes.

- Schedules.

- Admin tools.


Build Regulars first. Promote strong Regulars into Teams later.


### 3.5 Delay Payments


Payments introduce:


- Refunds.

- Disputes.

- Platform fees.

- Tax/compliance questions.

- Stripe Connect complexity.

- App Store policy complexity.


Keep cost note informational for now.


### 3.6 Reliability Score Needs Care


No-show tracking is valuable, but dangerous if too harsh.


Recommendations:


- Use private host-facing reliability first.

- Show positive reliability publicly.

- Avoid public "bad player" labels.

- Allow users to explain cancellations.

- Distinguish early cancellation from last-minute no-show.

- Do not punish weather, injury, host cancellation, or venue issues.


### 3.7 Chat Should Stay Cheap


Free tier chat should be:


- Text-only.

- Rate-limited for new conversations.

- No audio.

- No video.

- No file sharing.

- Profile images compressed.


This protects infrastructure while keeping social connection free.


### 3.8 Trust and Safety Is Not Optional


Offline meetups create real-world risk.


Before wider launch:


- Report user.

- Block user.

- Suspend user.

- Venue visibility controls.

- No exact personal home location.

- Basic community guidelines.

- Age/minor policy.

- Liability disclaimer.


### 3.9 The Best Moat Is Local History
